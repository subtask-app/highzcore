# Highzcore — Brand

The single source of truth for how Highzcore sounds, what we promise, and how we name the things we ship. Everything we write — product copy, marketing pages, error messages, support replies, bot messages, emails, tweets — derives from this document.

If something on the site contradicts this file, the file wins and the page gets fixed.

---

## 1. What Highzcore is, in one sentence

**Highzcore is a creator growth platform that connects YouTube creators with real, paid audiences in Southeast Asia and Africa for paid feedback, paid testing, paid promotion, and paid collabs — all backed by real data.**

If we ever need to say it shorter: *"Real audiences, real data, real growth."*

If we need to say it to a creator who's never heard of us: *"You upload your video, real people in your target audience watch it and tell you why it's not working, and you fix it before it flops."*

## 2. Positioning

**Who it's for:** YouTube creators in the 0–100k subscriber range who are stuck — uploading regularly but not growing, or growing slowly and unsure why. Most of them have tried sub services and been burned, or are about to. They have small budgets ($20–$500/video) and need every dollar to move the needle.

**The category we compete in:** Creator analytics & growth tools. Adjacent to TubeBuddy, VidIQ, 1of10, vidooly. Adjacent to focus-group services like UserTesting and PlaybookUX, but built for creators specifically and at one-tenth the price.

**The category we explicitly do not compete in:** Sub-farms, view-bot services, paid-engagement marketplaces. We are the opposite of these — we sell the diagnostic, not the cheat. Marketing copy must never blur this line.

**What only we can say:**

- We have a real, on-demand audience pool in the regions creators care most about reaching (SEA + West Africa — fastest-growing YouTube watch-time markets in the world).
- We deliver feedback at $50 that focus-group companies charge $5,000 for, because of where our workers are based — and we pay our workers competitively for their region.
- We give creators data they cannot get from YouTube Studio, TubeBuddy, or VidIQ: pre-publication feedback from their target demographic.

**The competitive narrative (we use this on comparison pages):**

> TubeBuddy and VidIQ tell you *what* is happening to your channel.
> Highzcore tells you *why* — and gives you specific things to change.

## 3. Brand voice

Five traits. Every piece of copy should hit all five.

### Clear

We say what we mean. We use plain words. We never make a creator look up a term to understand what we're offering.

**Do:** *"Find out what's wrong with your video before you publish it."*
**Don't:** *"Leverage demographic insight surfacing for pre-publication optimization."*

### Calm

Creators come to us anxious — their video flopped, their channel stalled, they're losing motivation. We never raise the temperature. No urgency manipulation. No "ONLY 24 HOURS LEFT!!!"

**Do:** *"Take your time. Your study runs until you have the responses you need."*
**Don't:** *"⚡ Don't miss out! Boost your channel NOW!"*

### Honest

We say what we can do and what we can't. We never promise a result we can't deliver (subscribers, views, ranking). We promise the *input* (real feedback from real people), not the *output* (you'll go viral).

**Do:** *"You'll get 50 honest answers from people in your target audience. What you do with them is the part that grows your channel."*
**Don't:** *"Get 10,000 subscribers guaranteed!"*

### Specific

Numbers, names, places. Vague reassurance is worthless.

**Do:** *"Average study completion time: 11 minutes. You get the first responses within 30 minutes of going live."*
**Don't:** *"Get insights fast!"*

### Warm

We're not a Silicon Valley enterprise SaaS. We're a platform serving creators in places where the gig economy is the main economy and YouTube is the second job. Be human. Be on their side.

**Do:** *"Hey — looks like your study filled up. Nice. Want to run another?"*
**Don't:** *"Your study has reached maximum response capacity. Initiate a new project to continue."*

## 4. Tone — how voice changes by context

Voice is fixed. Tone adapts.

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing pages | Confident, aspirational, but never hype | "Stop guessing why your videos don't grow." |
| First-time onboarding | Reassuring, friendly, hand-holding | "We'll set up your channel together — takes about 90 seconds." |
| Dashboard idle state | Calm, informative | "No active studies. Start one whenever you're ready." |
| Empty state | Encouraging without pressure | "Your first study lives here. We'll guide you through it." |
| Success | Brief, warm, no exclamation overload | "Study live. We'll text you when the first responses come in." |
| Error | Apologetic, specific, actionable, no jargon | "We couldn't reach your channel. Double-check the URL — should look like `youtube.com/@yourname`." |
| Payment error | Calm, clear, blame nothing on the user | "The payment didn't go through. No charge happened. Try again or pick a different method below." |
| Support | Conversational, first-person from a real human | "Hey, sorry that happened — I just refunded the study and added 20% credit to your account. Try again whenever." |
| Telegram bot | Concise, slightly more casual | "Got it. I'll ping you when your study fills." |
| Worker-side everything | Respectful, businesslike, payment-clear | "You earned $1.50 on this task. Payable to your wallet once you cross $10." |
| Legal pages | Formal but readable. Plain English wherever possible. | "If you violate these terms we may suspend your account. Before we do, we'll usually email you first." |

## 5. Audience-specific tone

We have two main audiences with different needs. We write to each differently.

### Talking to creators (paying customers)

They're solo entrepreneurs. They make money or want to make money on YouTube. They're time-poor and price-sensitive. They've been burned by hype.

- Use their language: "title", "thumbnail", "retention", "watch time", "CTR", "RPM" — they know these terms, we don't need to explain
- Show outcomes in their world: "your CTR will tell you which thumbnail is winning"
- Lead with the question they're already asking themselves: *"Why aren't my videos growing?"*
- Avoid corporate-speak: "users", "engagement metrics", "value proposition"

### Talking to workers (the audience pool)

They're earning supplemental income. They want clear instructions, fair pay, fast payouts, and to be treated with respect. Many will read in their second language.

- Use shorter sentences. Avoid idioms ("rule of thumb", "no-brainer", "ballpark") — they don't translate well.
- Always show the payment amount up-front, before the task description
- Show estimated time
- Never call them "labor", "human capital", "the workforce" — call them *workers* or *raters*
- Treat them like they're doing skilled work, because they are

## 6. Naming conventions

### Product names

These are the canonical names. Use them exactly. No abbreviations in marketing. Sentence-case in body copy.

| Canonical | Short form (URL slugs, code) | Don't say |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience Insights** | `insights` | "Surveys", "Polls", "Focus Groups" |
| **Thumbnail & Title Testing** | `abtest` | "Split testing", "A/B testing" (we own the simpler name) |
| **Promote** | `promote` | "Marketing", "Distribution", "Shilling" |
| **Collab** | `collab` | "Partnerships", "Influencer matching" |
| **Boost** *(if it ships)* | `boost` | Never on the main brand site |

### Other names

| What it is | What we call it | Don't call it |
|---|---|---|
| The video the creator uploads | *the video* | "asset", "content piece" |
| A request a creator places | *a study* (Insights), *a test* (ABTest), *a campaign* (Promote), *a match* (Collab) | "job", "project", "ticket", "order" |
| A response from a worker | *a response* (Insights), *a vote* (ABTest), *a share* (Promote) | "submission", "deliverable" |
| The person paying | *creator* | "client", "customer", "buyer" |
| The person doing the work | *worker* | "user", "evaluator", "participant", "audience member" |
| Their pay | *earnings* | "wages", "compensation", "tips" |
| Their wallet balance | *balance* | "wallet", "account", "credit" |
| Withdrawing earnings | *cash out* (UI), *withdrawal* (legal/admin) | "redeem", "claim" |
| The Telegram group for support | *Live Support* | "helpdesk", "ticket queue" |
| Our channel announcements | *Highzcore Channel* | "feed", "broadcast" |

### Slug & URL conventions

- All English-first URLs: `/insights`, `/abtest/new`, `/promote/campaigns/abc123`
- Locale prefix when localized: `/hi/insights`, `/id/abtest`
- Country landing pages: `/ng`, `/in`, `/id`, `/my`, `/sg`, `/gh` (lowercased ISO-3166-1 alpha-2)
- Never use underscores in URLs. Always hyphens.

## 7. Messaging do/don'ts (the hard rules)

### NEVER say

- "Buy subscribers"
- "Buy views"
- "Guaranteed subscribers"
- "Boost your channel" *(unless we're literally inside the Boost sub-brand product, separate domain)*
- "Beat the algorithm"
- "Hack YouTube"
- Anything that promises a specific metric outcome (subs, views, ranking position)
- "100% safe", "undetectable", "no risk" — these are sub-farm tropes
- Anything that compares us to sub-services
- Any language that sounds like get-rich-quick

### ALWAYS say (when relevant)

- "Real people watching your video"
- "Real, paid audiences"
- "Compliant with YouTube's terms"
- "Read-only access" (when describing OAuth scope — we read, we never post)
- Specific numbers, specific timeframes, specific demographics

### Trust language

Use sparingly and always with proof:

| Claim | Where it's allowed | Required proof |
|---|---|---|
| "Compliant with YouTube's terms" | Anywhere | Link to TOS page explaining our model |
| "Real people, not bots" | Anywhere | Link to worker verification page |
| "Verified workers" | Anywhere | Link to how-we-verify page |
| "Used by N creators" | After we hit N=50 real paying creators | Counter on homepage tied to DB |
| "Featured in [publication]" | Only with actual press coverage | Link to article |
| Testimonials | Only with permission, real name, real channel link | Signed permission |

## 9. Brand values

Five values. These show up in product decisions, hiring choices, support behavior, and what we refuse to ship.

### Real, not fake

We trade in real human responses, real audience reach, real collabs. The moment we'd need to fabricate to deliver, we don't deliver.

### Honest about limits

We tell creators what we can't do as clearly as what we can. *"This won't make a bad video good. It'll help you make a good video better."*

### Workers are workers, not metrics

Every worker is doing skilled, paid work. They get respect, fair pay, and clear payment terms. We pay on time. We refund refunds. We don't ghost.

### Slow is fine

We'd rather take 18 months to build a durable creator-services business than 3 months to build a paid-engagement business that gets banned. Patience over hype.

### Compliant is a feature

Compliance with YouTube's terms isn't a constraint we tolerate — it's a *feature we sell*. Creators are starting to learn that sub-services tank their channels. We are the boring, durable alternative. We lean into it.

## 10. Localization principles

We ship in 5 languages at launch: English (default), Hindi, Bahasa Indonesia, Bahasa Melayu, Tamil. Yoruba and Twi come post-launch.

- **English is the source of truth.** All copy is written in English first. Translations follow.
- **No machine-only translation.** LLM-assisted drafts are fine; every translation gets human review by a native speaker before it ships.
- **Voice survives translation.** Translators are briefed with this document. Voice + tone is non-negotiable in any language.
- **Locale ≠ country.** A Tamil speaker in Singapore uses the Tamil locale on the Singapore landing page. We don't pin a language to a country.
- **Number formats, currency, dates** — all locale-aware. `₦1,000` in Nigeria, `$10` in international view.
- **RTL** — not relevant for our launch languages. We design LTR-first.

## 11. Voice in the Telegram bot

The bot is part of the brand. It speaks in the same voice — clear, calm, warm — but more concise.

- Short messages. One idea per message.
- Use HTML formatting sparingly (`<b>`, `<i>`). Never MarkdownV2.
- One emoji max per message, and only when it adds clarity (✓, ⚠️, 💰)
- Always offer the user a clear next action ("Tap to open dashboard")
- Never use the word "AI" or pretend to be human — the bot is the bot

**Example bot message — task assigned:**

> 🎯 New task in Audience Insights
>
> <b>Watch this video + answer 7 questions</b>
> Estimated time: 11 minutes
> Payout: $0.85
>
> Tap to start →
